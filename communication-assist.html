About Motor Neuron Disease

Motor neuron diseases are a group of conditions that cause the nerves in the spine and brain to lose function over time. They are a rare but severe form of neurodegenerative disease.
- Medical News Today

  • Every two people per 100,000 are diagnosed with ALS.

  • Usually leads to death within 3-5 years.

  • Less than 5% survive for more than ten years.

Problem

People suffering from Motor Neuron Disease often have to sacrifice their communication abilities and have to rely on inefficient and poorly designed interaction technologies. Great technologies come with a hefty price tag that only a few can afford. Product User Experience in this area needs exploration and enhancement.

Solution

An innovative set of applications that can be used by a regular set of mouse and keyboard, touch, a trackball, and even an eye-tracking system. Users can go about selecting what they want to say, and the system will announce it via the speakers. Moreover, a host of accompanying applications makes everyday tasks more accessible.

Understanding communication problems

From Research

  • Loss of motor functions such as the ability to move body parts, speak, eat, sometimes over time, and sometimes all at once, leading to an inability to do everyday tasks.

  • Lots of communication issues due to loss of motor functions leading to an intense level of frustration and sometimes depression at initial stages.

  • Medical equipment usually costs a fortune and can put even a well-maintained family under debt.

  • Communication assistant devices are hard to learn since the patients cannot use their hands fully or partially.

From Caretakers of patients

  • "We ended up using a letter board like you see in breaking bad. It was very tedious and terrible.”
    - beardedjack (Reddit/r/ALS)

  • "My father had a trach put in right away so that was a problem. Then he used a stylus to point letter on a card. Towards the end, his hand had curled inwards so he had hard time holding the stylus. At the same time his hands were shaking a lot.”
    - syzygymoon (Reddit/r/ALS)

Learning about Competition

“There are many unique solutions in the market from inexpensive to highly expensive and sophisticated ones"

I looked into what kind of technologies hospitals and people are currently using with their patients and family members who have MND. Currently, hospitals and caretakers are using hardware boards with essential words written on them or computers with specialized hardware and software. A study from the University of Los Angeles shows that these technologies have a low satisfaction level. One reason is that it takes too much time to form sentences using these methods.

Research based Persona and Journey Map

“Some of the most inspirational stories and information about the lives of people with MND helped shape the persona and the journey map"

Based on what I learned from the research and findings about lives of people with motor neuron disease, I created a persona to better summarize their goals and frustrations. Based on the everyday life of people with ALS, I created a journey map to find the pain points and opportunities where a solution can be applied.

Conceptualizing new way of communication

“This product should be something they have to care the least to understand. On contrary, the product needs to care for them"

Vidatalk, a competing product that has been the inspiration of all the products currently out there, also served as baseline for this product as well. However, the point where most other products fail is to cater the user through their journey as the disease progresses. This means that it should allow them to use the product in multiple ways. To begin with, I designed a basic version just to test the idea with users. Being a special product, this needed a special usability testing scenario:

  • Two people for each session (one user and one friend).

  • Only the user was allowed to use the prototype.

  • Speaking was not allowed for the user.

Designing Visual Identity

Multiple modes of interaction

“A reason why many of the current solutions fail is that they neglect the second part of the interaction environment: the caretaker as a user"

The goal was to make it usable by not just one mode of interaction but multiple. That’s why all the interface elements are big enough to be easily seen from a distance and big enough to be touched.

A Meaningful and Friendly Identity

“The loading screen perfectly visualizes how Assistive technology has evolved to become smarter and how it is there to help them"

The first thing was to design the identity of the product to make it feel very friendly to the user and still convey the message about what the user can achieve out of the product. It starts with delight to the users’ eyes!

A comfortable Onboarding

“As soon as the user opens Communication Assist, it lets them experience what they can achieve with it"

Taking on the feedback from the usability study, I made the categories more prominent, a significant improvement over any other technology. Using real-world imagery is much easier to understand than using icons.

Composite Messages

“Why repeat a snippet of a message when the only change is a word?"

Composite messages ease the burden by allowing the user to quickly and easily select the word from available options. Plus, the AI automatically recognizes and adds options for the user when creating one with date, time, or activities.

Quick Select

“Having a quick selection of words and phrases allow users to save their most used phrases that can be accessed from wherever, regardless of app they are using"

The app allows the users to have a bunch of Quick Select messages which stay there no matter which app the user is currently using. Moreover, the Emergency button helps to quickly notify the caretakers when the user feels something is wrong.

Pain Rating

“What’s better than visually letting your caretaker know where and how much pain you’re having when you can’t speak or move most of your body?"

The app also allows the users to notify their caretakers if they are having any pain in their bodies. It reduces the immense amount of back-and-forth questioning with the user to find out where they are having the pain in their bodies.
