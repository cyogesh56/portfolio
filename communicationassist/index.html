Domain and Theme

The challenge was to create a communication interface or a device using radical technology. The first thing that came to my mind was Stephen Hawking. And, that’s when I decided that I would create something for people with ALS. As I started researching, I found that ALS is a part of a bigger hirearchy of diseases and comes under Motor Neuron Disease. All the diseases that come under MND lead to patients losing their ability to communicate.

Statistics of Motor Neuron Disease

  • Every 2 people per 100,000 are diagnosed with ALS.

  • Usually leads to death within 3-5 years.

  • Less than 5% survive more than 10 years.

  • Can only be diagnosed clinically.

Motor Neuron Disease: What actually happens?

Motor Neuron Disease (MND) affects the nerve cells that send signals to the brain. It is a progressive disease which means that it cannot be stopped from spreading but can be slowed won using medications. It damages the nerve fibers which weakens the muscles and the person gradually loses ability to move, speak and swallow.

Currently used MND communication methods

Currently, hospitals and caretakers are using hardware boards with important words written on them or computers with specialized hardware and software. A study from University of Los Angeles shows that these technologies have very low satisfaction level. One reason is that it takes a lot of time to form sentences using these methods.

Problems with current assistive methods

  • Inability to be used by the patient himself.

  • Figures not always represent the mental model.

  • Cartoonish look.

  • Expensive.

  • Bad UX.

  • Time consuming

Research

As I figured out the problems with current Assistive Technologies and Devices, it opened a path for a research in that channel. I looked on the internet about ALS but there is not much information about the lives of people with ALS. So, I asked on Reddit’s ALS forums on 'Communication problems of people with ALS'. Then I followed the traditional UX process to come-up with a prototype.

Routines of people with ALS (Persona)

There is not much on the internet about lifestyle of people with ALS probably because it is one of the most dreadful diseases and it’s very hard to talk about it. So, even a thorough Google search won’t give out lots of information. However, this lady, Linda Duffy was generous enough to provide a highly detailed description of her everyday life and struggle with ALS along-with many life lessons. It’s very saddening that she is no longer with us.

Click the image to follow the link to her daily routine. Alternatively, use this link: https://onmogul.com/stories/a-day-in-the-life-of-a-retired-teacher-diagnosed-with-als

Journey Mapping

After I collected all the information off the internet, I started analyzing it and I found that almost all of ALS patients have similar lifestyles. Since, Linda Duffy’s description was most detailed, I took her everyday journey as inspiration and added whatever was missing and created a journey map out of it.

Sketches

When I started this project, I had some idea of what kind of device and interface I want to create. So, I sketched my ideas and designed schematics of a sample device that can be created under $200. The device that I imagined would run on Raspberry Pi and the best thing about using an IOT device like Raspberry Pi is that it is one, a very loew cost device and two, it can be used to do a lot more than just using for the purpose.

Task Flow/Prototyping

After I sketched out what I want in a prototype, I used Axure to design a wireframe and I did a pilot testing on the wireframe. Based on the pilot test, I refined the design and made it hi-fi prototype.

Prototype Testing

After I completed the hi-fi prototype, I did another pilot test with a script to find out the best way of testing it with users since it’s extremely hard to find people with ALS given the timeframe I had to complete this project. Once I figured out all the logistics, I tested the prototype with 4 participants. That totals to 5 participants including a pilot test.

  • Speaking was not allowed.

  • Only one finger was allowed to use.

  • Gestures were not allowed.

  • Tried to mimic the actual scenario.

  • Follow-up questions.

Usability Test Results

The good

  • 4 out of 5 participants said that the prototype is easy to use.

  • 4 out of 5 participants said they will use this prototype.

  • 5 out of 5 participants clicked on the correct category for first 2 scenarios.

Things to improve

  • More accessibility friendly.

  • Ability to create conversations using predefined messages.

  • Inability to express pain easily.

  • Testing with real patients.
