Problem

Users are abandoning the Ventra app in favour of 3rd-party apps for transit information and navigation. They only use Ventra for managing their Ventra cards and passes. Yet, the intricate architecture and inefficient workflow of this app hinder users from efficiently doing so.

Solution

Find pain-points and understand the needs of everyday commuters by utilizing various user research methods, then create an overhauled app experience by following iterative user-centred design approach.

Result

A mobile and a wearable application design that leverages the best heuristics and usability standards to improve the workflow and remove roadblocks to help users manage their Ventra Cards and Metra Passes most conveniently.

About the CTA Ventra app

CTA helps 1.7 million daily weekday commuters in reaching their destination. Ventra is the companion app built for passengers to manage their Ventra Cards and purchase Ventra and Metra Passes. In addition to that, the Ventra app also provides the most accurate and up-to-date information about the trains and buses because it is maintained by the Chicago Transit Authority itself. However, our research says that most of these features are poorly designed and just barely serve the purpose on top of randomly crashing at crucial times. It needed a significant overhaul in user experience.

UX Process

Competitive Review

In our competitive review, we investigated several popular transit applications: Google Maps, Transit, Ventra, CityMapper and Transit Stop. These applications had features that Ventra did not have such as a map view, map and text view of routing, step by step commute information (e.g. est time to arrive, to walk, and disruptions). We noted down what each app does best, even the best features of Ventra itself.

Contextual Inquiry

We wanted to know how are the experiences of everyday commuters with CTA trains and buses. Being commuters ourselves, we used our knowledge to create the questionnaire. We asked participants about the applications they use for navigation if they have ever missed important announcements while riding CTA, how do they navigate to their destinations, and experiences around them. This helped us gather vital information about their commute problems and the reasons why people prefer 3rd-party apps over Ventra app for transit information.

Key takeaways from the interviews:

  • The primary way participants used the Ventra application was for fare management. Only 2 out of 6 participants were either aware of or have tried using Transit Information section in the app, but abandoned using the feature due to poor usability.

  • 5 out of 6 participants prefer using 3rd-party apps such as Google Maps and CityMapper as their primary source of transit information.

  • 3 out of 6 participants reported that they missed important announcements such as train becoming Express, halts and warnings while riding CTA.

Survey

After learning about commuters past experiences and future expectations, we decided to create a survey to get specific information from participants. We asked them about the app they regularly use to aid their commuting experience, for what purpose do they use that application if they ever missed essential announcements, etc. We gathered statistical evidence of the information we found through analysis, competitive review and interview sessions.

Prototypes and Usability Studies

We conducted 4 rounds of usability studies during this project, starting from the Ventra app itself to the Hi-Fi Prototype. We compared the scores on the System Usability Scale (SUS). We had a goal to achieve a SUS score of 70.

Ventra App Usability Test

The Positives:

  • All participants were successful in reloading their Ventra Cards with additional amount.

  • All participants found the current functionality to be adequate to their needs, but also wished for a more convenient way to operating the app.

  • No usability issues were found in the Metra section of the app.

The Negatives:

  • Participants disliked that adding a new card was buried under their account.

  • During our transit tasks, none of the participants were able to effectively plan a trip.

  • The SUS score for the baseline usability at 58.7 is lower than average and lower than our desired objective of 70.

Paper Prototype Usability Test

The Positives:

  • Mapping feature allowed participants to plan their trip which is a novelty over existing application.

  • All participants liked the new, more visual card management system and would prefer using it over Ventra application’s card management system.

  • The average SUS score for this test was a slightly better than the actual app, reaching to 62.

The Negatives:

  • Inconsistent terminologies sparked confusion among participants as they were not able to interpret the meanings without using that feature.

  • Absence of task flow created issues with information flow, making all screens disconnected from each other.

  • Participants felt as though there were too many confirmation messages and felt that this contributed to confusion.

Mid-Fi Prototype

The Positives:

  • All participants preferred using the call-to-action buttons from home screen to be able to quickly retrieve needed information.

  • All participants found the new card system helpful in managing their ventra cards and payment cards.

The Negatives:

  • Issues with the visibility of map made participants abandon the task or use 3rd-party apps.

  • Imprecise navbar terminologies still confused participants leading to higher task completion times.

  • Due to confusing terminologies and inability to complete certain tasks the SUS score for this round dropped to about 55.

Hi-Fi Prototype

Key takeaways from the Hi-Fi Prototype:

  • Streamlining the card management experience to the stock payment experiences on phones such as Apple Pay and Android Pay allows the user to bring-on their existing knowledge and manage their cards with ease.

  • Combining mapping functionality with CTA's most accurate, real-time transit updates would help in increasing the app retention rates, as found in the post-test questionnaire.

  • Providing users with timely and informative alerts will help them stay updated of their commute, Ventra Cards and any disruptions.
